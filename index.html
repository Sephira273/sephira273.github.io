Distance Modulus Equation

The distance modulus is one of the most commonly used equations in astronomy. Using the distance modulus, you can find out the distance (in parsecs) to any star or astronomical object if you know its apparent and absolute magnitudes.

(The apparent magnitude (m) of a star is how bright it appears to an observer on Earth, and the absolute magnitude is a measure of the star's intrinsic brightness, or luminosity).

Have a go on the calculator below, to find out the distances to Sirius, Alpha Centauri A, and the Andromeda Galaxy. (You can also think of your own).

  • Sirius: m = -1.47; M = 1.42
  • Alpha Centauri A: m = 0.01; M = 4.38
  • Andromeda: m = 3.44; M = -21

Wien's Displacement Law

Wien's displacement law is a physical law that states that the peak wavelength of a radiating black body is inversely proportional to its temperature.

In astronomy, this law can be used to work out the peak wavelength of a radiating body like the Sun given its surface temperature (and vice versa).

Use the calculator below to work out the peak wavelength of the light emitted by the following stars:

  • The Sun: 5778 Kelvins
  • Sirius: 9940 Kelvins
  • Alpha Centauri A: 5790 Kelvins
  • Gliese 581: 3480 Kelvins
  • TRAPPIST-1: 2550 Kelvins

You can also try using the calculator to find out the peak wavelengths of some other objects:

  • The human body: 310 Kelvins
  • The cosmic microwave background: 2.73 Kelvins

The Habitable Zone

The habitable zone, or circumstellar habitable zone, is the range distances around a star where a planet can have its orbit and be able to sustain liquid water on its surface (and thus support life).

The Earth is within this range of orbits in our solar system (towards the outer edge), at a distance of about 150 million km from the Sun. This distance is defined as 1 AU (1 astronomical unit).

The habitable zone of any solar system is determined by the luminosity of its star, (though the native atmosphere of a planet is also a factor, which is ignored by this equation).

The calculator below will show you the average orbital radius (in AU) of the habitable zone for any star that you input. Try using it with the following data for stars that have been found to have planets orbiting them inside the habitable zone:

  • Gliese 581: Luminosity = 0.013

Redshift & Recession Velocity

All stars emit electromagnetic radiation. Astronomers can analyse their electromagnetic spectra, and use that data to determine whether a star (or galaxy of stars) is moving away from us, or towards us.

Stars that are moving away from us show spectral lines that are shifted towards the red part of the spectrum (in comparsion to their reference wavelengths) - we say that they are 'redshifted' - and stars that are moving towards us have spectral lines that are shifted towards the blue, or 'blueshifted'.

Distant galaxies and quasars are given a 'z' number, and this z number represents their redshift (or blueshift). Positive z numbers indicate an object whose light has been redshifted, and negative z numbers indicate an object whose radiation has been blueshifted.

A positive z number (redshift) means that an object is moving away from us, and a negative z number (blueshift) means that it is approaching.

The following calculator can take the redshift of an object (for z < 1), and determine the speed with which it is approaching (shown as a negative velocity) or receding (shown as a positive velocity).

  • Andromeda Galaxy: z = -0.001001
